### 1. Fundamental Tense Transformations

The most critical aspect of voice is the systematic shift of auxiliary verbs across different tenses. While the Active Voice focuses on the doer, the Passive Voice emphasizes the action and the recipient.

- **Mechanism:** In passive voice, the verb "to be" is added in the appropriate tense, followed by the past participle (V3) of the main verb.
    
- **Key Rules:**
    
    - **Simple Tenses:** "Plays" becomes "is played"; "played" becomes "was played."
        
    - **Continuous Tenses:** These require the addition of "being" (e.g., "is washing" becomes "is being washed").
        
    - **Perfect Tenses:** these require the addition of "been" (e.g., "has finished" becomes "has been finished").
        
    - **Note:** Future Continuous and all Perfect Continuous tenses are generally not used in the passive voice.
        

**Recall Chain:** Mechanism → Simple → Continuous → Perfect

---

### 2. Transitive vs. Intransitive Verbs

Voice transformation is only possible when a sentence contains a direct object. This distinction determines whether a sentence can even exist in the passive form.

- **Logic:** A sentence can only be made passive if there is an object to move into the subject position.
    
- **Key Rules:**
    
    - **Transitive Verbs:** Verbs like "eat," "write," or "buy" take objects and can be transformed (e.g., "He wrote a book" $\rightarrow$ "A book was written").
        
    - **Intransitive Verbs:** Verbs like "sleep," "go," or "laugh" do not take objects and cannot be passive. You cannot say "The bed was slept by him" in standard grammar.
        
    - **Prepositional Objects:** Sometimes intransitive verbs followed by a preposition can be passive (e.g., "They laughed at him" $\rightarrow$ "He was laughed at").
        

**Recall Chain:** Logic → Transitive → Intransitive

---

### 3. Imperative Sentences (Commands and Requests)

Imperative sentences do not have a visible subject (the implied subject is "you"), requiring a specialized structure for passive conversion.

- **Structure:** Most imperatives use the "Let" formula or a "You are requested/ordered" prefix.
    
- **Key Rules:**
    
    - **Standard Commands:** "Close the door" becomes "Let the door be closed."
        
    - **Negative Commands:** "Do not kill the bird" becomes "Let the bird not be killed."
        
    - **Advice/Requests:** If the sentence implies advice, use "should be" (e.g., "Help the poor" $\rightarrow$ "The poor should be helped"). If it is a request, use "You are requested to..."
        

**Recall Chain:** Structure → Commands → Advice

---

### 4. Sentences with Dual Objects (Direct & Indirect)

Many sentences contain two objects: a person (indirect) and a thing (direct). These sentences can be written in two different passive forms.

- **Logic:** Either the person or the thing can become the new subject of the sentence, depending on what the speaker wants to emphasize.
    
- **Key Rules:**
    
    - **Indirect Object as Subject:** This is usually more natural (e.g., "He gave me a pen" $\rightarrow$ "I was given a pen by him").
        
    - **Direct Object as Subject:** Requires a preposition like "to" or "for" (e.g., "A pen was given **to** me by him").
        
    - **Retention:** The object not chosen as the subject is called the "retained object."
        

**Recall Chain:** Logic → Indirect → Direct

---

### 5. Interrogative Sentences (Questions)

Transforming questions into passive voice involves maintaining the interrogative word order while applying voice rules.

- **Logic:** The auxiliary verb must still appear before the subject to keep the sentence a question.
    
- **Key Rules:**
    
    - **Yes/No Questions:** "Did he see you?" becomes "Were you seen by him?"
        
    - **'Wh-' Questions:** "Who" changes to "By whom" (e.g., "Who wrote this?" $\rightarrow$ "By whom was this written?").
        
    - **'Whom' Questions:** "Whom" changes to "Who" (e.g., "Whom did you invite?" $\rightarrow$ "Who was invited by you?").
